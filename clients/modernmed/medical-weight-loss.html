The basics

What medical weight loss is.

Weight treated as a medical condition — with medicine and oversight, not willpower alone.

Medical weight loss is a physician-supervised program that treats excess weight as a medical condition rather than a personal failing. It can combine an FDA-approved medication with laboratory testing, eligibility review, and ongoing monitoring.

The medications most often used are GLP-1 receptor agonists such as semaglutide. These are prescription medicines, and whether they are appropriate for you — and at what dose — is determined clinically by your physician after reviewing your history and results.

This is medicine with a clinician behind it: a real evaluation, real monitoring, and a plan matched to you — not a one-size-fits-all program from a website.
How it works

How GLP-1 medications work.

The plain-language version of what semaglutide and related medications actually do.

They mimic a natural hormone

GLP-1 is a hormone your gut already releases after eating. These medications act like it — helping regulate appetite and blood sugar through pathways your body uses every day.

Less hunger, slower emptying

In plain terms, they reduce hunger and cravings and slow how quickly the stomach empties, so you feel full sooner and longer. That makes eating less feel less like a constant battle.

Effect and timeline vary from person to person. Your physician explains what to expect for you, and adjusts the plan over time.
Who it's for

For people where other approaches fell short.

If diet and effort alone haven't gotten you there, that doesn't mean you failed — it may mean you need a clinical approach.

Adults who've tried before

Many patients have worked hard with diet and exercise and still struggled to reach or hold their goals. A medically supervised program adds tools that effort alone can't provide.

Eligibility determined clinically

Suitability isn't decided by a number on a page. Your physician reviews your medical history, current medications, lab results, and goals to determine whether GLP-1 treatment is appropriate for you.

Safety

Side effects & physician monitoring.

Effective medicine, used responsibly — with a physician watching the details.

What to expect

GLP-1 medications are FDA-approved and widely used, but they are prescription medicines. The most common side effects are gastrointestinal — such as nausea — and often ease as the dose is adjusted gradually.

Physician oversight throughout

Your physician reviews your history before you start, monitors you over time, manages side effects, and adjusts the plan. This is supervised care — not a medication mailed without a clinician.

Tell your physician about all medications and conditions. GLP-1 treatment isn't right for everyone, and your clinician will discuss the benefits, risks, and alternatives with you.

Questions

Medical weight loss, answered.

What is medical weight loss, and how is it different from a diet program?

Medical weight loss is a physician-supervised program that treats weight as a medical condition. It can combine FDA-approved GLP-1 medications such as semaglutide with laboratory testing, eligibility review, and ongoing monitoring — rather than relying on willpower or a commercial diet plan alone.

How does semaglutide (a GLP-1 medication) work?

GLP-1 medications mimic a natural gut hormone that helps regulate appetite and blood sugar. In plain terms, they slow how quickly the stomach empties and reduce hunger and cravings, which can make it easier to eat less without constant effort. Your physician explains the expected effects and timeline for you.

Who is a candidate for GLP-1 weight loss treatment?

These programs are generally for adults who have struggled to reach their goals with diet and exercise alone. Eligibility is determined clinically, based on your medical history, current medications, lab results, and goals reviewed at your consult — not by a number on a website.

Are GLP-1 medications safe, and what side effects should I expect?

GLP-1 medications are FDA-approved and widely used, but they are prescription medicines that require physician oversight. The most common side effects are gastrointestinal — such as nausea — and often ease as the dose is adjusted. Your physician reviews your history, monitors you over time, and manages side effects throughout treatment.
